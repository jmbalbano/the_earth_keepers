Week 1 · Waste Reduction
From Simple Tasks to Meaningful Lessons
Our first week of waste reduction showed that simple actions, like my choice to reuse food containers, Richyll bringing reusable bags, Jea practicing waste segregation, and Jahzara carrying a reusable water bottle, can lead to meaningful changes in awareness and habits. Although Richyll felt awkward using reusable bags in plastic-heavy spaces and Jea found carrying an eco-kit inconvenient, these experiences taught us to be more mindful of our consumption. Jahzara’s struggle to use both sides of paper reflected how sustainability often means choosing responsibility over convenience or appearance. Through these practices, we learned that consistency is what turns small actions into lasting habits. In the end, this week showed us that embracing simple, imperfect efforts can reduce waste, support others, and give us pride in helping the environment.

Week 2 · Energy Conservation
Conscious Routines, Smaller Footprints
Throughout the week, the activities we performed show that saving energy mainly comes down to being more conscious of our everyday routines. Our experiences with turning off lights and unplugging devices highlight how easy it is to waste electricity on autopilot, and how small actions require discipline at first but eventually become natural. Our use of natural lighting and reduced screen time reflect practical decisions, proving that even simple adjustments in daily habits can already lessen energy consumption. At the same time, using power-saving modes and setting timers for air conditioners show that technology can also support conservation when used properly, despite minor inconveniences like reduced performance or comfort. Our experience with using sunlight to dry clothes adds another perspective, showing that conserving energy can also mean maximizing available natural resources.

Week 3 · Water Conservation
Small Steps Toward Water Wisdom
Throughout the week, the activities we performed demonstrate that it is simply a matter of being more conscious of daily routines in order to save water. John's experience highlights how easy it is to waste water on autopilot, and how small adjustments like turning off the tap or shortening showers take discipline but eventually become manageable. Jea's center around practical decisions and it is evident that even without a lot of complexity simple decisions such as taking a bucket or reporting leaks can already help. Jahzara's reflections point out how convenience often leads to waste, yet how routines can be modified with a little more effort, which will make things seem less wasteful in the long run. Richyll's part adds another perspective by using rainwater, showing that conservation can also involve making use of available resources, while still being mindful of possible downsides. In general, the third week demonstrates that saving water cannot be done in one big step, but in small steps, becoming more aware of the everyday activity until these small steps become natural.

About This Project

Our Mission

A four-week journey of environmental stewardship, one documented act at a time.

Why "The Earth Keepers"?

A keeper is not just someone who owns something — it is someone who protects it, tends to it, and ensures it endures. We chose this name because we believe the Earth does not belong to us; we belong to it. As Earth Keepers, we have to treat the planet with the deliberate care it deserves. Every reusable bag carried, every faucet turned off, every piece of litter picked up is an act of keeping: small in scale, but sincere in intention. We keep the Earth not by saving it in a single act, but by choosing it — again and again, every day.

The Earth Keepers is an initiative by four environmental stewards committed to practicing and documenting genuine acts of environmental care.

Each week, every member took on a daily environmental challenge — from reducing plastic waste and conserving energy, to mindful water use and building sustainable habits. The goal wasn't perfection; it was consistency, awareness, and growth. We documented not just what we did, but how it felt, what was hard, and what changed in us.

This website is our living record — a testament that environmental stewardship doesn't require grand gestures. It begins with small, intentional choices made every single day.
